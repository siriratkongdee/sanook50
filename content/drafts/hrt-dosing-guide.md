Most women on HRT are flying blind.

Their doctor said "you should feel better" — and maybe they do. But feeling better is not the same as being dosed correctly. The gap between those two things is where the real damage happens: bone loss, cardiovascular risk, and metabolic dysfunction quietly accelerating while your symptoms feel manageable.

I found this out the hard way. My April bloodwork came back with testosterone at 112 ng/dL. The reference range is 2–45. I was running at 2.5 times the upper limit, and nobody caught it.

---

## What the Experts Say

### 1. Estradiol has a target range — and most women aren't hitting it

Dr. Louise Newson, UK menopause specialist, is clear: you need estradiol above 250 pmol/L to get the health benefits — bone protection, cardiovascular protection, cognitive protection. Below that, you're getting symptom relief but not the systemic benefits. Above 1,000 pmol/L, you're in excess territory.

The problem is absorption varies wildly between women. Some need higher doses of gel or cream just to reach therapeutic levels through the skin. The only way to know is to test — 12 hours after your last application, not right after.

### 2. "Normal" labs can mask real dysfunction

The Kresser Institute makes a point that stopped me cold: estradiol at 293 pg/mL looks completely normal on paper. But if your cycle is running 18–46 days, something is wrong. A single lab snapshot doesn't tell the whole story.

The same is true for testosterone. Total testosterone can look "normal" while free testosterone — the fraction actually available to your tissues — is low. If your SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin) is above 100, it's binding up your hormones and leaving you deficient despite the numbers looking fine. Oral estrogen raises SHBG. This is a hidden variable most standard panels miss.

### 3. The testosterone/belly fat paradox nobody talks about

Here's the finding that surprised me most, and I've been sitting with it for weeks.

The SWAN Study tracked 359 women over four years and found that higher testosterone is associated with *more* visceral fat in women — not less. Visceral fat (VAT) increases 3.8% annually during menopause. Women with higher testosterone accumulate more of it, independent of age, total body fat, or insulin resistance.

This is the opposite of what happens in men.

Testosterone does increase lean mass and reduce subcutaneous fat — the fat under your skin. But it drives visceral fat accumulation — the metabolically active fat packed around your organs. At physiologic doses (35–45 ng/dL), the tradeoff may be worth it. At supraphysiologic levels above 70–100 ng/dL, you're getting the worst of both: VAT accumulation plus lipid harm.

### 4. Route and type of hormones change your lipid outcomes

A 2022 meta-analysis of 73 studies found that estrogen alone improves your lipid profile — lower LDL, lower total cholesterol, lower Lp(a), higher HDL. Good news.

But add a synthetic progestin, and those benefits are blunted. Micronized progesterone (bioidentical) is significantly better than synthetic versions. Route also matters: oral estrogen gives a bigger HDL boost but raises triglycerides. Transdermal drops LDL and Lp(a) without the triglyceride spike.

If your lipids are worsening on HRT, the first three questions are: What type of progestin are you on? What's your delivery method? And what's your testosterone dose?

### 5. Early initiation has a 44% cardiovascular protection effect — but only if you start in the window

Peter Attia calls menopause a metabolic crisis, not a natural transition to ignore. His data point: women who start HRT within 10 years of menopause onset show a 44% reduction in coronary disease risk. Start too late, after atherosclerosis has already progressed, and the timing advantage is lost.

FSH rising above 10–12 during days 3–5 of your cycle is an early warning signal. Most women don't test this. Most providers don't order it.

## Siri's Take

I've been on HRT for about a year. My symptoms improved. I felt better. I assumed that meant my doses were right.

They weren't.

My April 2026 labs: testosterone at 112 ng/dL, reference range 2–45. That's a 2.5× overdose. At the same time, my LDL climbed 52% and my HDL dropped 18% compared to October 2024. I was watching my lipids deteriorate in real time and couldn't figure out why.

The research explains it. Excess testosterone drives visceral fat accumulation. Visceral fat drives insulin resistance. Insulin resistance drives lipid dysregulation. It's a cascade, and it starts with a dose that's too high.

The bitter irony: testosterone is often prescribed to women for energy, libido, and body composition. And at the right dose, it probably helps with all three. The optimal range is 35–45 ng/dL for total testosterone. I was at 112. I wasn't getting more benefit — I was getting metabolic harm.

I also wasn't being monitored. Nobody ordered free testosterone or SHBG. Nobody reran my lipids at six weeks when I changed my dose. Comprehensive follow-up was not standard practice — I had to ask for it, and I didn't know to ask until I'd already seen the damage in my labs.

I'm an AWS architect. I build distributed systems for a living. I know what happens when you don't monitor your systems: they drift, they degrade, and eventually something breaks. HRT is no different. You have to treat it like an engineering problem — set targets, measure inputs and outputs, adjust, and retest.

"You should feel better" is not a monitoring protocol.

## One Action

**Get a comprehensive hormone and metabolic panel — not just the basics.**

If you're on HRT (or considering it), this is the panel that actually tells you what's happening:

**Hormones:**
- Estradiol (test 12 hours after gel/cream application)
- Progesterone
- Total testosterone
- Free testosterone
- SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin)
- Free Androgen Index (FAI)

**Metabolic / cardiovascular:**
- LDL, HDL, triglycerides
- ApoB
- Lp(a)
- hsCRP (inflammation marker)

Retest 6–8 weeks after any dose change. Don't wait six months to find out something drifted.

If your provider won't order free testosterone and SHBG, ask directly. If they can't explain why your lipids changed since starting HRT, that's a conversation you need to have — or a provider you need to replace.

You are not asking for permission. You are managing your own system.
